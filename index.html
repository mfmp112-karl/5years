The honest premise

Nobody gives partnerships. They are earned by building something the other party needs to be associated with. The pathway below is not about pitching these organisations. It is about becoming the kind of company they reach out to.

The arm
Media
Podcast, daily content, newsletter, live events. Builds the audience. Earns public credibility. Gets Rory Sutherland's attention.
The arm
Tools
Free Google Sheets, paid SaaS systems built on Anthropic's API and Notion's platform. This is the technical integration story for Anthropic and Notion.
The arm
Education
Cohorts, certification, facilitator system. Measurable SME transformation. The data that gets Google and Hormozi to pay attention. 10,000 SMEs helped is the number.

The three-arm architecture is the right structure for this ambition. Each arm feeds a different partnership conversation simultaneously. You are not choosing between being a media company, a SaaS company, and an education company. You are building all three — and the partnership opportunities emerge from each arm at different points in the five years. The work is not to pitch. The work is to build each arm to a threshold where the pitch writes itself.

Part II
Each partnership in full

What each organisation needs, what you offer them, and the exact steps to get there.

🤖
Anthropic — Claude
Integration partner · API-powered SME tools
Year 2–3

Anthropic wants to show that Claude's API is creating real economic value in underserved markets. A Kenyan SME platform built on their API — with measurable business outcomes — is exactly the kind of story their partnership and policy teams need for African market credibility, press coverage, and investor narratives.

What you offer them: a documented use case of Claude enabling 1,000+ African micro-businesses to operate more intelligently, with published outcome data. That is not something they have. You build it. They associate with it.

The exact steps
01
Year 1: Get an Anthropic API key. Build the first tool using Claude — the Business X-Ray diagnostic. Even a simple implementation counts. Document it publicly.
02
Year 2: Reach 200 monthly active users on the API-powered tool. Apply to Anthropic's startup programme (currently called Anthropic for Startups). It gives API credits, which reduces your costs significantly.
03
Year 2–3: Publish a case study: "How Marketing FM uses Claude to help Kenyan SMEs diagnose their business in 15 minutes." Submit it to Anthropic's team directly. Tag them publicly when it goes live.
04
Year 3: Apply for Anthropic's formal partner programme with 500+ active users, a published case study, and measurable outcomes. The application is now a formality — the relationship has been built through usage and content.
05
Year 3–4: Co-branded content — "Claude-powered tools for African SMEs" — appears on both Marketing FM's channels and Anthropic's. This is the partnership made visible.
📋
Notion
Integration partner · Template creator · Certified consultant
Year 2–3

Notion wants to grow in emerging markets — Africa specifically represents a massive untapped user base of exactly the kind of knowledge workers and small business owners who benefit most from Notion's product. Marketing FM is the natural distribution channel for that growth because it already owns the relationship with the audience Notion wants.

What you offer them: an active community of East African SME owners who use Notion as their business operating system — because Marketing FM built that system for them, teaches it through cohorts, and certifies people in it. Notion gains market penetration without building a sales team in Africa.

The exact steps
01
Year 1: Build the Business HQ template publicly and give it away free. Every SME who downloads it becomes a Notion user because of Marketing FM. Track this number obsessively.
02
Year 2: Pass the Notion Certified Consultant exam. Cost is approximately $200 USD. This is a credential, not a partnership — but it opens the formal relationship. Submit the Business HQ template to Notion's public gallery.
03
Year 2: Reach 1,000 template downloads. This is Notion's internal threshold for taking template creators seriously as partners. At 1,000 downloads you appear in their data — without downloads you do not exist.
04
Year 3: Apply to Notion's Ambassador programme. Required: active community, certified status, documented usage, content track record. At this point all four conditions are met.
05
Year 3–4: Build the Claude-in-Notion integration — the three-way product. This becomes the flagship partnership story: African SMEs running their businesses inside Notion, powered by Claude, managed through Marketing FM's system.
🔍
Google
Google for Startups Africa · Workspace ecosystem · SME digitisation
Year 3

Google for Startups Africa actively backs entrepreneurs with cloud credits, mentorship, and visibility — but the selection criterion is demonstrated traction, not early-stage promise. They want to accelerate businesses that are already moving, not fund businesses that are trying to start. This is why Year 3 is the right entry point.

What they offer you: Google Cloud credits (significant reduction in SaaS infrastructure costs), access to Google's African market network, and the Google badge — which makes every enterprise sales conversation and every subsequent partnership conversation significantly easier.

The exact steps
01
Year 1–2: Use Google Workspace products visibly and publicly. Google for Startups is more receptive to applicants already in their ecosystem — Workspace, Google Sheets, Google Forms, Google Meet.
02
Year 2: Apply to Google Hustle Academy as a training partner or collaborator. This puts Marketing FM inside Google's African SME training network at a lower threshold than the Startups programme.
03
Year 3: Apply to Google for Startups Africa with: 1,000 SMEs served, job creation data, East Africa expansion, and a SaaS product with paying users. These are the criteria their selection team evaluates.
04
Year 3–4: The "set up workspaces in Kenya" vision — Google funding SME workspace setup through Marketing FM as the implementation partner — is a Year 4 conversation, once Google for Startups has validated the model.
📈
Alex Hormozi
Meeting · Not a partnership · Acquisition.com ecosystem
Year 4–5

Hormozi does not take meetings from people who reach out to him. He meets people who build something he considers worth publicly acknowledging. His team curates case studies for his content — businesses that used his frameworks and reached a scale he considers impressive. The pathway to meeting him is through that curation process.

Marketing FM has a structural advantage here that most of his audience does not: you are applying his frameworks in a market he has never operated in, serving a customer profile he has never coached directly. A Kenyan SME operating on the Hormozi Scaling Roadmap, generating KSh 8M/month, run by a 23-year-old founder — that is a story his content team actively wants.

The exact steps
01
Year 1–3: Document every Hormozi framework you use, publicly, with real results. Not "I applied the value equation" — "I applied the value equation to a Kenyan pastry business and here is what changed in 30 days." This is the content his team searches for.
02
Year 3: Submit Marketing FM as a case study to Acquisition.com directly. Their website has a submission process. The threshold is approximately $1M ARR or a documented transformation story with real numbers.
03
Year 4: At KSh 3M/month, engage actively in Hormozi's community channels — Skool, YouTube comments, his events. Build a track record of substantive, documented contributions. His team notices people who show up consistently with real results.
04
Year 4–5: The meeting happens. It is not scheduled through an assistant. It is the result of three years of public building in his framework, documented results that his team found interesting, and being in the right room at the right time.
🧠
Rory Sutherland
Meeting · Ogilvy · Behavioural economics in African context
Year 3–4

Rory Sutherland is more accessible than any other person on this list. He genuinely engages with interesting ideas on X and LinkedIn. He responds to people who send him real research. He has cited obscure bloggers and small researchers in his talks when they offered him a genuinely new angle. The pathway to him is the shortest of all five targets — probably 18–24 months if the right content is built.

The angle that gets his attention: behavioural economics as applied to African micro-businesses. Rory's entire intellectual project is about how psychology drives economic decisions that conventional economics cannot explain. African SME behaviour — the M-Pesa psychology, the credit normalisation, the trust-based commerce, the word-of-mouth mechanics — is a goldmine of exactly the kind of irrational but logical behaviour he writes about. Nobody is studying this through his lens. That gap is the opportunity.

The exact steps
01
Year 1: Start writing a newsletter or LinkedIn series called something like "The Irrational Kenyan Business." Apply Rory's framework — obliquity, psycho-logic, signalling — to real Kenyan SME behaviour. One piece per week. This costs nothing.
02
Year 2: Turn the best pieces into a short research document. Real data from Marketing FM's community — how Kenyan SMEs actually make pricing decisions, why they resist tracking data, what actually drives referrals. Publish it publicly as a PDF.
03
Year 3: Send Rory the research directly on X with a single sentence: "I've been applying your framework to Kenyan micro-businesses — I think you'll find this surprising." No pitch. No ask. Just the link and the reason it might interest him.
04
Year 3–4: The conversation happens. His interest in the African behavioural data leads to a public acknowledgement — a retweet, a mention in a talk, a citation in a piece. That acknowledgement is the Ogilvy connection made visible.

The thing that kills most plans like this is not the cost and not the work — it is the 4-month dead zone in Year 1. You will post content, coach clients, build tools, and share the podcast for 90–120 days and see almost no visible external response. The community will be quiet. The downloads will be low. The revenue will feel fragile. This is not failure. This is what every compounding business looks like before the compound kicks in. The founders who get to Year 3 are the ones who did not interpret the dead zone as a signal to stop or pivot. They interpreted it as the price of entry and kept building.

The free stuff question — yes, a lot of it. In Year 1 and Year 2, approximately 80% of what Marketing FM produces is free. Free tools. Free podcast. Free WhatsApp content. Free Notion templates. This is not charity — it is audience-building, which is the asset that makes every other thing on this list possible. The partnerships, the SaaS revenue, the cohort fees, the sponsorships — all of it flows through an audience that trusts Marketing FM. You cannot buy that trust. You build it, one free useful thing at a time, until the people who receive it decide they want to pay for what comes next.
